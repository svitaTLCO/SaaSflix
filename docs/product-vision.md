# Product Vision

## Core definition

This platform is a **creator-led software studio membership platform**.

Subscribers do not join for one isolated product. They join to access the founder's growing universe of apps, experiments, launches, and discussions. The platform must feel like a living digital studio: part product ecosystem, part launch venue, part private members club, part community around software in motion.

## What the product is

The platform combines five systems into one coherent experience:

1. **Membership platform**  
   Accounts, plans, billing, entitlements, profile, access control.
2. **App library and launcher**  
   A unified catalog of all published software products.
3. **Drop and release system**  
   Launch pages, changelogs, previews, beta access, roadmap motion.
4. **Community and support layer**  
   Product-native discussions, feedback, issue visibility, answers, inspiration.
5. **Creator feed**  
   The founder's updates, thinking, development notes, announcements, and direction.

## What the product is not

It is not:

- a generic marketplace,
- a normal SaaS bundle with shallow links,
- a feature request board disguised as a community,
- a support portal bolted onto a storefront,
- or a random collection of unrelated side projects with no narrative.

## Product promise

The user promise is:

- one membership,
- one identity,
- one coherent software universe,
- continuous access to released products,
- visibility into what is being built next,
- and a sense that the platform is alive.

## Product pillars

### 1. Authored universe
Every part of the platform should feel intentionally authored. The product reflects a worldview and a creative direction, not commodity software assembly.

### 2. Continuous motion
The platform should feel active even between major launches: release notes, beta expansions, feed posts, roadmap shifts, experiments, community insights.

### 3. Product-native community
Community must revolve around products, releases, support, and ideas. Avoid generic social features that do not strengthen product usage or platform identity.

### 4. Trustworthy membership
Users must feel confident that payment, access, account safety, and product availability are handled professionally.

### 5. Honest lifecycle visibility
Each product must clearly communicate whether it is concept, in development, beta, stable, experimental, or archived.

## Primary user motivations

Users may subscribe for different reasons. The platform should support all of them without confusion:

- access to useful apps,
- curiosity about new launches,
- alignment with the founder's taste,
- participation in an inner-circle software club,
- early access to experiments,
- community and conversation around the products.

## Plans and membership logic

Use three plans by default unless strategy changes:

- Base
- Pro
- Ultra

The plans should not be framed only as "more features." They should be framed as **depth of access and participation**.

Possible differentiation model:

### Base
- access to stable published apps
- standard feed and community participation
- baseline support and account features

### Pro
- access to beta products and advanced product capabilities
- earlier drops
- stronger participation in roadmap and feedback loops

### Ultra
- earliest access to experiments
- premium community proximity
- higher limits
- stronger founder-circle privileges

The exact commercial policy may evolve, but the product architecture must support tiered entitlements cleanly.

## Major product surfaces

### Public surfaces
- landing page
- pricing
- about / philosophy
- app previews
- public launch pages where appropriate
- changelog excerpts
- account entry points

### Member surfaces
- home feed
- app library
- app detail pages
- launch pages
- roadmap / upcoming
- support and discussions
- notifications
- profile / security / billing

### Admin surfaces
- app registry management
- release publishing
- moderation
- support oversight
- subscription diagnostics
- analytics
- feature flags
- content operations

## Product statuses

Every app and release should support visible lifecycle states:

- Concept
- In Development
- Private Beta
- Public Beta
- Stable
- Experimental
- Deprecated
- Archived

These states should influence UI treatment, expectations, access, support policy, and discoverability.

## Strategic north star

The platform should become the place where a user thinks:

> "I subscribe because this world keeps evolving, and everything inside it feels like it belongs together."

## Design consequences

Because the platform is creator-led, the UX should emphasize:

- launch moments,
- visual identity,
- progression over time,
- personal voice without becoming chaotic,
- product discovery without store-like clutter,
- and emotionally resonant presentation of software.

## Technical consequences

Because the platform will host many products over time, the architecture must support:

- centralized identity
- centralized entitlements
- centralized app registry
- attachable support/community threads
- configurable launch metadata
- analytics by app, release, plan, and user cohort
- feature flags and staged access
- future native product integration

## Success criteria

The platform is succeeding when:

- users understand the platform within minutes,
- multiple products feel like parts of the same world,
- launches feel meaningful,
- support and community reduce friction instead of creating noise,
- and the infrastructure can support ongoing shipping without chaos.
